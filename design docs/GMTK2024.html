Theme: Built to Scale

Make a game that emphasizes size and dimension. Teeny tiny. Planetary. Big becomes small, vice versa.

Given Examples:

  • Katamari Damacy
  • Superliminal
  • Osmosis
  • Everything

Brainstorming

Flowers for Algernon-like

Narrative focused. Start small, become big, and have to become small again. Would have to keep scope tight. Would need to figure out some mechanic to actually make it a game.

Narrative Options

  1. Map events of Flowers for Algernon more directly. As in book, more tragic tone, great gain but inevitable loss.
  2. More game-like & getting bigger enables you to complete different tasks. Narrative focus on greed and power.
  1. Stealing and gaining wealth in an illegal way to a point. Then having/gives it all back eventually.

Mechanics

  1. Mini-game puzzles

Putting things that “scale” onto a weight scale - SELECTED

Idea is to put items i.e, packages onto a scale and record weight. The items start at a perceived normal weight and as the game goes on the items get heavier and bigger. We could fug with density and have big items be very light or a small item that's very heavy.

Related games: Papers Please, Contraband Police, Little Inferno

Scaling mountain

  1. Endless hallway - the size of the player or room changes to make it look like the end destination is continually out of reach

Related game: Jusant

Dragon Scales

Player picks up scales which makes them heavier and slower, but stronger. If they pick up too many heavy scales they can’t move anymore.

Judgment Scale 1

You are responsible for making decisions which become increasingly significant. Each decision will have different effects on meters that should stay balanced. Possibly corporate or political setting.

Inspired in part by: Reign

Judgment Scale 2

You have to decide who gets into heaven but there are limited spots and you don’t know who will come next. Really good people! Really bad people! If you do a bad job you get fired by St Peter?

Inspired in part by: crazy shit in Persona

Snowball

You start as a little snowball rolling down a mountain and get bigger and bigger. You may start subsuming large structures. Don’t get broken I guess would be the challenge? So it could be a score-based infinite runner?

Inspired in part by: Donut County, Katamari Damacy, this asset pack

Design: Package Fulfillment

Brainstorming item Putting things that “scale” onto a weight scale.

Working Title: Untitled Package Fulfillment Game

Other ideas: <your idea here xoxo>

Overview

What do you do?

Get delivery requests shown to you and find the right item in the package bay for that order. This is done by determining the weight of each package from a scale.

What is the aesthetic/setting? Or as Noah would put it, what’s the canon?

You are a fulfillment agent at some warehouse helping to assign packages to the people who placed orders. Industrial, grimy, a little futuristic.

How does the theme apply?

Using a scale is a core mechanic of the game. Aside from weighing things, the items being weighed have a great variety of masses. One item could be a feather (ex: 0.008 grams), another could be a whole star (ex: 1.9891 × 1030 kilograms).

What makes the game a challenge?

As the game progresses, the “catalog” of possible items increases with various weights giving the player more choices to pick from increasing the chance of messing up a delivery as well as having to spend more time having to find the right package. The time in between when a new package would show up would decrease, forcing the player to complete orders faster to make sure that there is not a backup of packages. This difficulty in the game would happen as more orders are done correctly.

How does the game end? Win or lose? How is progress marked?

The game ends and you lose when there are too many packages to be held in the package bay. There is not necessarily a “win” state, though maybe if the entire catalog is discovered with a threshold of so many deliveries of each item. Progress is marked by fulfilling deliveries to unlock more items in the catalog. A Scoring System may also be used.

Components

Prioritization

  • Green Light: components which are essential to the game.
  • Yellow Light: components that if things are going well we can put it/work on it. Think of this as a form of polish.
  • Red Light: components that should not be or will not be added due to time or technical constraints. “juicing” the game.

Package Bay

The shelves. The place that holds packages you have to fulfill. Has a maximum capacity.

Packages

This is what the player will pick up and move. These will have weights assigned to them.

Variance from container weight

Different types of containers (wooden crate vs cardboard box) will have different weights. This adds a little bit of mental math to figuring out what’s in the package.

Scale

Spot where the player will place the packages down to determine the weight of said package through a display of some kind.

Sticky Weights

Once a package has been weighed, its weight should stay visible.

Catalog

These are the different types of items that will be in the packages. Each item needs a name and a weight that is reasonably close to what the item would really weigh.

Catalog Progression

Fulfilling orders for an item grants you extra skill. After reaching a threshold of deliveries for each item, packages arriving in the package bay containing that item are enhanced.

Tier 1

You’ve got a good sense of the weight. The package weight is automatically revealed.

Tier 2

You’re pretty certain what this is. The item’s weight is revealed in the catalog.

Tier 3

You’re a god among warehouse employees. There is a chance the item in the package is automatically revealed. Chance increases with fulfillments of this item.

Items

Items will appear as cards in the catalog. Each item has a realistic corresponding weight that will be used to determine what order it should be placed in. Items should be ordered and grouped by when they become available in the catalog.

  • Sunglasses - 20 g
  • The moon - 7.35e22 kg
  • Television - 20 kg
  • Sun - 1.9891 × 1030 kg
  • Mobile Phone - 140 g
  • Shirt - 113 g
  • Paper clip box - 15 g
  • 8x10 Tibetan Carpet - 22 kg

Orders

This is how the game progresses. The more orders that are done correctly means that the catalog increases in size and the time between packages showing up will decrease. Making incorrect orders will punish the player by giving the player more packages for a short time.

If there is a Scoring System implemented this is where the score would get determined.
Correct orders = points, incorrect orders = penalty, quick fulfillment = more points

Submitting Orders

User drags a package to an order to send it out for delivery. Some time will pass (not necessarily long, <<10s). The sent order will then be added to the Scoring System, tracking how many orders have been completed.

Unit Conversion

Let the user select if they want the weights in imperial or metric

Saving Weights from the Catalog

Once the player weighs a package, that knowledge will then be saved for when that package is brought onto the shelf again. This is done through a section of the UI so the player can quickly look there and possibly complete an order without needing to use the scale at times.

Scoring System

This is for bragging rights. Whoever has the best score among your friends is just better.

Customer Reviews

A la Cookie Clicker, some nice polish would be a section where “customer reviews” can come in. Positive comments for completed orders, negative for incorrect shipment. Can be varied by fulfillment time. These should be silly.

Scene & Object Implementation Notes

I am approximating a technique called Model-View-ViewModel. It is fairly common for applications. Essentially, there is a Model which holds the data, the View presents the data, and the ViewModel handles interactions.

UML-ish Diagram

20140

Above is a diagram intended to show how the different structures are related. It’s mostly just an overview and omits many details. Chief among the omitted details is the game logic and how it flows through instances of the entities. I made some oopsies so I tried to fix them in the Docs Drawing. I will briefly explain how to read the diagram, but first a

Note

In some cases, relationships may be more theoretical. For instance, the diagram shows the PackageManager “owns” the Packages. I do not expect the PackageManager singleton will have all Package nodes as its children. They will mostly be under the PackageBay, which the diagram shows as having connections to Packages. In the end, the PackageManager will be the entity responsible for the whereabouts of the Packages.

Reading the diagram

I will be paraphrasing for convenience. I also haven’t done this shit in like 4 years so I’m just trying my best.

Boxes

Each box represents a type like a node, scene instance, resource, or object. Green boxes indicate the type is in part or wholly for UI. Purple boxes are singleton logic nodes that well… manage parts of the game. Blue boxes are mostly just data structures, where instances of them are like variables of a string or an image. It’s important to note that the boxes only represent things that can be created, not instances of those things.

Lines

The lines show relationships. The diamonds show which side holds the reference to the other side, at least conceptually and organizationally. The filled-in diamond represents an “owning” relationship. The entity with the diamond “owns” instances of the entity on the other end of the line. The hollow diamond represents a dependency. The entity with a hollow diamond depends on a reference to instances of the entity on the other end. (It’s more complicated than this. It’s generally more like “owns the reference to” than “depends on”. I promise this is different from “owns”.)It is important to note that these relationships may exist but might not always exist. For instance, an Item can exist in the CatalogManager and Catalog, but does not necessarily have any connections to Orders or Packages.

Numbers and Stars

These show the cardinality of the relationships or, in other words, the amount in the relationships. The expression “0..1” means there can be zero or one, “1” means there is exactly one, and “*” means there can be any number. (Usually “*” necessarily includes zero, but I was lazy in cases where it’d realistically always be more than zero. The proper way to write this is “1..*”)

If a “1” is by box X and a “*” is by box Y, this means an instance of X can have any number of connections to instances of Y, but across all instances of Y’s connected to X’s there is in fact only one X to which Y’s can be connected.

Examples: All Together Now

Take Package and Scale. A Scale can own a reference to 1 Package, but it might have no (0) connection to a Package. A Scale does not “own” its Package, and is thus not responsible for its lifetime. There is also only ever 1 Scale that a Package could be connected to.

Take GameManager and CatalogManager. The GameManager owns the CatalogManager, meaning the lifetime for the CatalogManager is bound to the lifetime of GameManager. The GameManager has 1 CatalogManager, and a CatalogManager can only be connected to one GameManager.

Retrospective

This design didn’t see a very strong implementation. I failed to anticipate that the pace of development would require more organic design. That’s ok.
